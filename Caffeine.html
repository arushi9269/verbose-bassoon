Caffeine and its Effects

Each day more than 2 billion people worldwide consume caffeine in some form. This natural stimulant is one of the most commonly used ingredients in the world. Discussions are often held regarding the ill effects of caffeine consumption. However recent studies show that it has various health benefits. This article talks about the effect of caffeine on your health.


What is Caffeine?

Caffeine is a central nervous system stimulant of the methylxanthine class. It is bitter in taste; white in colour. It is chemically related to adenine bases of deoxyribonucleic acid or DNA. Although a psychoactive drug, it is legal in nearly all parts of the world. It is found in the seeds, nuts, or leaves of a number of plants native to Africa, East Asia and South America like coffee, tea and cocoa. It helps protect these plants against predator insects and prevents germination of nearby seeds. It has an effect on the brain and nervous system, helping you to stay alert and preventing dizziness. It has both positive and negative impacts on health

How does it prevent tiredness?

Caffeine once consumed in quickly absorbed into the bloodstream. From there it reaches the liver and is broken down into smaller compounds that affect various organs.

IN THE BRAIN: Adenosine is a neurotransmitter which relaxes the brain and thus makes you feel sleepy. Caffeine reversibly blocks the action of adenosine on the body and consequently prevents the onset of drowsiness. Dopamine (another neurotransmitter) is associated with emotions like pleasure and motivation. On the other hand, norepinephrine increases the blood pressure and heart rate. Caffeine increase the activity of these two neurotransmitters and this combination further stimulates the brain and promotes a state of arousal, alertness and focus.

Additionally, caffeine shows its effects in a short period of time. The effects reach peak levels within 30 to 60 minutes of its consumption. This is the time you’re most likely to experience the “jittery” effects of caffeine. Caffeine’s half-life is up to 5hrs. this means that if you’ve consumed 10mg of caffeine, after 5 hours, you’ll still have 5 mg of caffeine in your body.

In what form does one consume caffeine?

If you’re looking for a quick jumpstart in the morning or you’re trying to stay alert at work in the afternoon it is natural to reach for a cup of coffee or tea. Although coffee may be one of the world’s most popular sources of caffeine it is certainly not the only way to get a boost of energy in one’s daily routine. Here are the amounts of caffeine expected per 240 ml of some popular beverages:

  • Espresso: 240–720 mg (milligrams)
  • Coffee: 102–200 mg.
  • Energy drinks: 50–160 mg.
  • Tea: 40–120 mg.
  • Chocolate milk: 2–7 mg.
  • Sodas: 30-40 mg
Some foods also contain caffeine-
  • 1 oz of milk chocolate: 15 mg.
  • 1 oz od dark chocolate: 30 mg.
  • Ice cream and frozen desert: 35 mg.
  • Breakfast cereals: 11 mg.
  • Pudding: 10 mg.

    • The following doses have been studied in scientific research for adults:

    1. General: One cup of brewed coffee provides from 95-200 mg of caffeine.
    2. For headache: Does of 100-250 mg caffeine daily have been used. Caffeine has also been used together with acetaminophen, aspirin, ergotamine, and sumatriptan.
    3. For mental alertness: Doses of 100-600 mg caffeine daily have been used. Caffeine has also been taken together with taurine, glucose and L-theanine.
    4. For asthma: Caffeine has been taken in doses of 9 mg/kg.
    5. For improving athletic performance: 2-10 mg/kg has been used.
    6. For preventing gallstone disease: Consumption of 400 mg or more of caffeine daily has been used.
    7. For memory: 65-200 mg of caffeine has been used.
    8. For pain: Doses of 50-130 mg caffeine have been used with pain relievers including acetaminophen, propyphenazone, and ibuprofen.

    Which all parts of our body are affected?

    Caffeine has no nutritional value and is tasteless. Some food or medications might contain caffeine without your knowledge. Knowing the symptoms of caffeine and its long-term effects on your body may make you think twice about having that fourth cup. Read on to learn more about these effects.

    • Caffeine acts as a natural stimulant. As told earlier, the most noticeable effect when it reaches the brain is alertness. You feel more awake and less tired.
    • Latest studies have found that people who drink coffee regularly have a lower risk of developing Alzheimer’s, Dementia and Parkinson’s by 28-60%
    • Also, caffeine is often taken along with pain relievers like aspirin for treating migraines, tension headache and post-surgery headaches. ,/li>
    • Combining caffeine with glucose as an "energy drink" seems to improve mental performance better than either caffeine or glucose alone.
    • Taking 200 mg of caffeine by mouth daily seems to improve memory in some people with outgoing personalities and college students.
    • Caffeine increases amount of acid in stomach and may cause heartburn or an upset stomach.
    • Excess caffeine isn’t stored in your body. It is processed in the liver and exits through your urine. So, you might experience an increase in urination after having caffeine.
    • In case you suffer from ulcers, doctors must be asked about whether it is safe for you to have caffeine or not.
    • Drinking beverages that provide at least 400 mg of caffeine daily seems to reduce the risk of developing gallstone disease.

    Caffeine can have wide ranging health benefits, only if consumed in a controlled manner. Keeping a check on your caffeine intake can lead to a healthy life.
